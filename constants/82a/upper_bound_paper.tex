\begin{abstract}
Doche's perturbed-polynomial construction produces upper bounds for the essential
minimum $\essmin$ of the Zhang--Zagier height by adjoining integer
\emph{perturbing blocks} to a base family and tuning real exponents; which blocks
lower the bound, and how far the construction can be driven, had been determined by
search. We give the construction an analytic foundation. Doche's limit value is a
ratio $\log h=\Phi(q)/D(q)$ in which the numerator is an integral of a pointwise
maximum and the denominator a maximum of affine functions; both are non-smooth, so
the existence of the derivative is not automatic. We prove that $\log h$ is
right-differentiable in each block exponent and compute the first variation in
closed form, uniformly across the three regimes of which branch attains the
denominator---including the tie, where the derivative has a genuine one-sided kink.
The load-bearing analytic point, the vanishing of the moving-boundary term, is
settled by dominated convergence on the difference quotient. From this single
derivative a firing criterion follows: a block lowers the bound to first order
according to a sign condition on a single arc integral, with the condition taking one
form for a base block (no value term) and another for a perturbing block (the
complementary-slackness condition $\rQ=\log h$ of the associated measure linear
program). The criterion accounts for the constructions found by search. Used as a
design objective it selects further admissible blocks, certifying an enriched family
that improves on \citet{Doc01b}; and applied to the recent record of \citet{Gri26},
obtained by an independent denominator-first search, it fires on \emph{every} block of
that construction---including two new near-cancellation factors our analysis had not
seen---while staying inert on generic blocks, and the record's active blocks lie on
the criterion's stationarity locus. The two constructions are the same Doche
functional, and the search is the criterion's calculus carried out numerically. We
also give computational evidence---an additivity property of the marginal with an
explicit finite search---that within a specified finite dictionary the cheaply
improving blocks have already been absorbed. Code at \repo.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%==============================================================================

For $\alpha\in\Qbar$ let $h(\alpha)$ be the absolute logarithmic Weil height. The
\emph{Zhang--Zagier height} \citep{Zha95,Zag93} is $\hZ(\alpha)=h(\alpha)+h(1-\alpha)$,
and its \emph{essential minimum}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:def}
  \essmin:=\sup\bigl\{\,H\in\R:\ \{\alpha\in\Qbar:\hZ(\alpha)\le H\}\ \text{is finite}\,\bigr\}
\end{equation}
is the largest threshold below which only finitely many algebraic numbers lie. Its
value is unknown. The lower record is $0.2487458=\log1.282416$ \citep{F18}; on the
upper side \citet{Doc01b} long held $0.25443677=\log1.289735$, recently improved by a
denominator-first search of \citet{Gri26} to a certified $0.2536331090$. Thus
\[
  0.2487458\ \citep{F18}\ \le\ \essmin\ \le\ 0.2536331090\ \citep{Gri26}.
\]
\citet{BMQS} have shown that the two classical methods producing these bounds are
linear-programming duals with no duality gap, so the residual interval is a
truncation gap, not a structural one.

The upper bound is built by an explicit limit-point construction of
\citet{Doc01a,Doc01b}. One fixes a base family of integer polynomials and a
distinguished block, and forms a family of spectrum limit points by adjoining
\emph{perturbing blocks} $Q_k\in\Z[X]$ ($X=z(1-z)$) with nonnegative real exponents.
Every admissible choice yields a valid bound $\essmin\le\log h$, and one drives the
bound down by tuning the exponents and enlarging the dictionary of blocks. Two
questions have been answered only empirically: \emph{which} integer polynomials lower
the bound when adjoined, and \emph{how far} the construction reaches. This paper
answers both by differentiating the construction.

\subsection*{The functional and its first variation}
Doche's value is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ratio}
  \log h(q)=\frac{\Phi(q)}{D(q)},\qquad
  \Phi(q)=\int_0^1\max(A,B)\,ds,\qquad
  D(q)=\max(\dA,\dB),
\end{equation}
where $A,B$ are real combinations of $\log|R(\chi(s))|$ over the active polynomials,
$\chi(s)=z(1-z)$ with $z=e^{2\pi is}$, and $\dA,\dB$ are the two arguments of the
denominator's maximum, affine in the exponents (Section~\ref{sec:functional}). The
numerator integrates a pointwise maximum and the denominator is a maximum of two
affine functions; neither is smooth, and the first obstacle is simply to show the
derivative exists.

\begin{theorem}[First variation; Sections~\ref{sec:firstvar}--\ref{sec:unified}]
\label{thm:main}
Let $Q\in\Z[X]$ have no root on the contour $\chi([0,1])$, adjoined to one branch of
an admissible family with exponent $q_Q\ge0$, under the standing hypotheses
\emph{(H1)--(H2$'$)} of Section~\ref{sec:firstvar} (in particular $A_0-B\not\equiv0$).
Then $\log h$ is right-differentiable at $q_Q=0$, and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:main}
  \dplus\!\log h/\partial q_Q\big|_0
  =\frac1D\Bigl[\int_{\ACT}\log|Q(\chi(s))|\,ds-\log h\cdot s_Q\Bigr],
\end{equation}
where $\ACT$ is the arc on which $Q$'s branch attains the inner maximum, and
$s_Q=\deg Q$ if that branch attains $D$ at $q_Q=0$ and $s_Q=0$ otherwise. The
identity holds in all three regimes $\dA\gtrless\dB$ and at the tie
$\dA=\dB$; at the tie $\log h$ is not two-sided differentiable, the left and
right derivatives differing by $\log h\cdot\deg Q/D$.
\end{theorem}

The proof has two parts. The inner derivative
$\int_{\ACT}\log|Q(\chi)|\,ds$ is obtained by dominated convergence on the difference
quotient (Theorem~\ref{thm:firstvar}); its only inputs are a $1$-Lipschitz bound and
the finiteness of the switching set, so it is \emph{independent} of which branch
attains the outer maximum. The denominator's one-sided derivative is the affine
max-rule, which supplies the term $s_Q$ and, at a tie, the kink
(Theorem~\ref{thm:unified}). Everything below flows from \eqref{eq:main}.

\subsection*{Consequences}
Specializing \eqref{eq:main} to a perturbing block isolates a selection rule.

\begin{corollary}[Selection criterion; Section~\ref{sec:criterion}]\label{cor:criterion}
Take a family in which the perturbing branch attains $D$ (the regime of the
constructions below), and adjoin a block $Q$ at exponent $0$. Then $Q$ lowers the
bound to first order according to which branch it joins:
\begin{itemize}
\item a \emph{base} block ($Q$ on the $A$-branch, the losing argument) fires iff its
  \emph{base marginal} is negative,
  $\;\widetilde r_Q:=\int_{\{A_0>B\}}\log|Q(\chi(s))|\,ds<0$;
\item a \emph{perturbing} block ($Q$ on the $B$-branch, which attains $D$) fires iff
  its \emph{normalized active potential} is below the value,
  $\;\rQ:=\frac1{\deg Q}\int_{\{B>A_0\}}\log|Q(\chi(s))|\,ds<\log h$.
\end{itemize}
The two tests are the two cases $s_Q\in\{0,\deg Q\}$ of \eqref{eq:main}. At a family
satisfying the first-order conditions, the perturbing form gives the
complementary-slackness reading of the \citet{BMQS} measure program restricted to
Doche's family: each active perturbing block sits on $\rQ=\log h$, and an inactive one
has nonnegative reduced cost $\rQ-\log h$.
\end{corollary}

The base test has \emph{no} $\log h$ term, because a base block enters the argument
that does not attain $D$; it is the test that selects the blocks of the enriched family
of Section~\ref{sec:bound}. Run as a design objective over integer blocks, either test
bounds the construction.

The criterion's main use is descriptive: it identifies which blocks any Doche-family
construction should carry, the record constructions among them.

\begin{theorem}[The criterion accounts for the record; Section~\ref{sec:record}]
\label{thm:record}
The current record bound $\essmin\le 0.2536331090$ of \citet{Gri26}, obtained by a
denominator-first search, is the same Doche functional \eqref{eq:ratio} written in
complementary coordinates. Its denominator carries six perturbing blocks, among them
two new near-cancellation factors $R_0,R_2$ outside the dictionary studied here. At the
candidate-free anchor the perturbing criterion fires on \emph{every} one
($\rQ<\log h$, strictly), while generic integer blocks of like degree stay inert; and
at the record's own optimum the active blocks lie on the stationarity locus
$\rQ\approx\log h$ of Corollary~\ref{cor:criterion}. The record is thus the criterion's
calculus carried out by search, not a fortuitous find.
\end{theorem}

As an independent illustration, used as a design objective on the dictionary of this
paper the base criterion selects two further admissible blocks; certifying the
resulting family gives $\essmin\le0.2538893183$, improving Doche's longstanding
$0.25443677$ (Theorem~\ref{thm:upper}, Section~\ref{sec:bound})---short of the
\citet{Gri26} record but obtained from the criterion rather than from search.

\begin{theorem}[An improvement on Doche; Section~\ref{sec:bound}]\label{thm:upper}
$\essmin\le 0.2538893183$, strictly below the longstanding $0.25443677$ of
\citet{Doc01b}, certified by an outward-rounded interval-arithmetic enclosure.
\end{theorem}

Finally, run as a design objective the criterion indicates where the construction's
easily reachable gains run out. The base marginal $\widetilde r_Q$ is additive over
factorizations in $\Z[X]$, and an explicit finite search finds that the cheaply firing
blocks have already been absorbed into the dictionary (Section~\ref{sec:saturation}).
This is computational evidence, not a non-existence theorem: high-degree admissible
blocks outside the search can still fire (we exhibit table entries that would), so we
claim only that the cheaply \emph{reachable} improvements within the searched dictionary
are absorbed, and explain the mechanism.

\subsection*{Scope}
Theorem~\ref{thm:main} is a \emph{local} first-variation theory: it computes the
derivative at $q_Q=0$ for Doche's functional, not a statement about $\essmin$ itself.
Theorem~\ref{thm:record} is an \emph{explanatory} statement: the criterion fires on,
and accounts for, the blocks of the \citet{Gri26} construction---it does not
\emph{generate} the near-cancellation factors $R_0,R_2$, which are that author's
construction, but it certifies after the fact that they satisfy its firing and
stationarity conditions, and that generic blocks do not. The improvement of
Theorem~\ref{thm:upper} is genuine but small, and is no longer the record; its role is
to confirm that the analysis selects blocks predictively, from the criterion rather
than from search. The saturation discussion of
Section~\ref{sec:saturation} is computational evidence over an explicit finite search,
\emph{not} an unconditional non-existence statement; we are precise there about what is
proven (additivity, the finite enumeration) and what is not (no claim over all integer
blocks). We do not claim block selection is a transfinite-diameter problem---it is not,
and we record the obstruction (Remark~\ref{rem:noteq}). Every assertion is backed by a
re-runnable computation; Section~\ref{sec:verify} collects these, keyed by statement
number, so that no claim rests on a number that cannot be reproduced.

\section{The Doche functional}\label{sec:functional}
%==============================================================================

If $\alpha$ is an algebraic integer of degree $d$ with minimal polynomial
$P\in\Z[z]$, then $\hZ(\alpha)=\frac1d\log\bigl(M(\alpha)M(1-\alpha)\bigr)$ with $M$
the Mahler measure. We work in the symmetric coordinate $w=z(1-z)$, invariant under
$z\mapsto1-z$; on the unit circle $z=e^{2\pi is}$ we write
$\chi(s)=z(1-z)=e^{2\pi is}-e^{4\pi is}$, $s\in[0,1]$. The Green function is
$g(z)=\log^+|z|+\log^+|1-z|$ with $\log^+x=\log\max(1,x)$. By
\citet[Thm.~6.5]{BMQS}, $\essmin$ equals the infimum of $\int g\,d\mu$ over
conjugation-invariant probability measures $\mu$ with $\int\log|Q|\,d\mu\ge0$ for all
$Q\in\Z[x]\setminus\{0\}$.

Doche realizes such measures as limiting Galois-conjugate distributions of a
perturbed-polynomial family \citep{Doc01a,Doc01b}. Fix base polynomials
$\{P_1,P_2,P_4,P_6,P_8\}\subset\Z[X]$ and a distinguished block $Q_1Q_2\in\Z[X]$ of
degree $56$ (the \citet{Doc01b} record family; $Q_1,Q_2$ its degree-$28$ factors).
The exponents are real because they are \emph{limit proportions}: \citet[\S3]{Doc01a}
realizes a nonnegative real exponent vector $q$ as the limit, along a sequence of
integer polynomials $\prod_i P_i^{n_i}\prod_k Q_k^{m_k}$ with $n_i,m_k\in\Z_{\ge0}$, of
the normalized multiplicities $n_i/N,\,m_k/N$ as $N\to\infty$ through rational
approximations $n_i/N\to q_i$; the spectrum value \eqref{eq:ratio} is continuous in
these proportions, so every $q\in\R_{\ge0}^{\,\cdot}$ is attained as a limit point and
the bound $\essmin\le\log h(q)$ holds for real $q$. Adjoining a base or a perturbing
block enlarges this index set by one coordinate and preserves the construction.
For exponents $q$ over the base and $\{q_k\}$ over perturbing blocks $Q_k\in\Z[X]$
(all $\ge0$), the limit value is \eqref{eq:ratio} with
\begin{equation}\label{eq:AB}
  A(s)=\sum_i q_i\log|P_i(\chi(s))|,\qquad
  B(s)=\log|Q_1Q_2(\chi(s))|+\sum_k q_k\log|Q_k(\chi(s))|,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Dformula}
  \dA=\textstyle\sum_i q_i\deg P_i,\qquad
  \dB=56+\sum_k q_k\deg Q_k .
\end{equation}
The form $\max(A,B)$ is the inner Jensen integral of Doche's double integral, and
\eqref{eq:Dformula} is the \citet[\S4]{Doc01a} $D$-formula. A block may be adjoined to
the \emph{base} branch (entering $A,\dA$) or the \emph{perturbing} branch
(entering $B,\dB$).

\begin{lemma}[{\citealp[Lemmas 2--5]{Doc01a}}]\label{lem:doche}
For any admissible family, $\log h(q,\{q_k\})$ is a limit point of the spectrum
$\{\hZ(\alpha)\}$; in particular $\essmin\le\log h(q,\{q_k\})$ for every admissible
choice. We call a family \emph{admissible} when the hypotheses of \citet[Lemmas
2--5]{Doc01a} hold; the following conditions are \emph{sufficient} for admissibility
and are the ones we verify: the distinguished block has positive degree, each $Q_k$ is
squarefree, the active product $W$ (of all factors with positive exponent) satisfies
$W(0)=W(1)=1$, and the factors of $W$ are pairwise coprime and coprime to the base,
which gives Doche's nontriviality condition $\prod P^n\ne\prod Q^n$ by unique
factorization. A base block need not satisfy $P(0)=P(1)=1$ (e.g.\ $P_1=X$), whereas a
perturbing block, entering the distinguished product, does.
\end{lemma}

No optimality of the exponents is needed for the bound; the analysis below exploits
this by enriching the dictionary.

\section{The first-variation lemma}\label{sec:firstvar}
%==============================================================================

Fix an admissible family with branches $A_0,B$ as in \eqref{eq:AB}, and adjoin a
block $Q\in\Z[X]$ to the \emph{base} branch with exponent $q_Q\ge0$, so
$A(s,q_Q)=A_0(s)+q_Q\log|Q(\chi(s))|$ while $B$ is unchanged. Write
$\Phi(q_Q)=\int_0^1\max\bigl(A_0(s)+q_Q\log|Q(\chi(s))|,\,B(s)\bigr)\,ds$. We assume:

\begin{itemize}
\item[(H1)] $Q$ has no root on $\chi([0,1])$, so $\log|Q(\chi(\cdot))|$ is bounded,
  hence in $L^1[0,1]$;
\item[(H2)] $A_0,B$ are real-analytic off the finite set of contour roots of their
  constituent polynomials, with integrable $-\infty$ logarithmic singularities there;
  near each such point $A_0\to-\infty$, placing a neighbourhood in $\{A_0<B\}$;
\item[(H2$'$)] $A_0-B\not\equiv0$ on each analytic sub-arc (the branches are not
  identically equal; verified for the families here, where $\{A_0>B\}$ has positive
  measure $<1$);
\item[(H3)] the perturbing branch attains $D$: $\dA(0)<\dB$, so $D\equiv\dB$
  is locally constant in $q_Q$.
\end{itemize}

\begin{theorem}[First-variation lemma]\label{thm:firstvar}
Under \emph{(H1)--(H3)}, $\Phi$ is right-differentiable at $q_Q=0$ with
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Phiprime}
  \Phi^{+\prime}(0)=\int_{\{A_0>B\}}\log|Q(\chi(s))|\,ds,
\end{equation}
and consequently $\dplus\!\log h/\partial q_Q|_0=\Phi^{+\prime}(0)/D$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For $q_Q\ne0$ set $\psi_q(s)=\bigl[\max(A_0+q_Q\log|Q|,B)-\max(A_0,B)\bigr]/q_Q$,
evaluated at $\chi(s)$.

\emph{Domination.} The map $t\mapsto\max(a+t,b)$ is $1$-Lipschitz, so
$|\psi_q(s)|\le|\log|Q(\chi(s))||$; by (H1) this dominator lies in $L^1[0,1]$
uniformly in $q_Q$, including at the contour-root singularities of $A_0$, where
$\max(A_0,B)=B$ for both $q_Q$ and $0$ so $\psi_q=0$.

\emph{Pointwise limit off the switching set.} Let $K=\{s:A_0(s)=B(s)\}$. For
$s\notin K$ and $s$ not a contour root of $Q$: if $A_0(s)>B(s)$ then the first branch
dominates for small $|q_Q|$ and $\psi_q(s)\to\log|Q(\chi(s))|$; if $A_0(s)<B(s)$ then
$\psi_q(s)\to0$. Thus $\psi_q\to\log|Q(\chi)|\cdot\mathbf 1_{\{A_0>B\}}$ a.e.\ off
$K$.

\emph{The switching set is null.} On each of the finitely many closed sub-arcs into
which the contour-root set (H2) divides $[0,1]$, $A_0-B$ is real-analytic and, by
(H2$'$), not identically zero; a real-analytic function $\not\equiv0$ has isolated
zeros, and isolated zeros in a compact arc are finite in number, so $K$ is finite,
hence null. The contour-root singularities lie in $\{A_0<B\}$ (H2), not in $K$.

\emph{Conclusion.} Dominated convergence gives
$\Phi^{+\prime}(0)=\lim_{q_Q\to0^+}\int_0^1\psi_q\,ds
=\int_{\{A_0>B\}}\log|Q(\chi)|\,ds$. The moving boundary of the active set needs no
separate treatment: the a.e.\ limit is already the fixed-set integrand
$\mathbf 1_{\{A_0>B\}}$, and dominated convergence equates the limit of the quotient
with its integral. The first-order boundary term vanishes \emph{because $A_0=B$ on
$K$}---the envelope cancellation---not because the boundary is fixed. Finally
$\log h=\Phi/D$ with $D$ constant near $0$ by (H3).
\end{proof}

The vanishing of the moving-boundary term is the substance of the lemma: a direct
``differentiate under the integral and add a boundary term'' argument must establish
it by hand, whereas here the $1$-Lipschitz bound and the finiteness of $K$ let
dominated convergence absorb it, with the $-\infty$ singularities of $A_0$ controlled
by the same dominator. This is the Danskin/envelope phenomenon for a parametric
integral of a maximum \citep{Ber99}, made rigorous for the present non-smooth
integrand.

\section{Removing the regime hypothesis}\label{sec:unified}
%==============================================================================

Theorem~\ref{thm:firstvar} assumed (H3), that the perturbing branch attains $D$. We
remove it. Since $\dA,\dB$ are affine (the $D$-formula \eqref{eq:Dformula}),
$D=\max(\dA,\dB)$ is convex and piecewise-linear, with one-sided derivatives
everywhere. At $q_Q=0$ exactly one of three regimes holds:
$\dA(0)>\dB$ (I), $\dA(0)<\dB$ (II), or $\dA(0)=\dB$ (III, a tie).

\begin{theorem}[Unified one-sided marginal]\label{thm:unified}
Let $Q$ be adjoined to side $X\in\{A,B\}$ with exponent $q_Q\ge0$, under
\emph{(H1)--(H2$'$)}. Then $\log h$ is right-differentiable at $q_Q=0$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:unified}
  \dplus\!\log h/\partial q_Q\big|_0
  =\frac1D\Bigl[\int_{\ACT(X)}\log|Q(\chi)|\,ds-\log h\cdot s_Q\Bigr],
\end{equation}
where $\ACT(A)=\{A_0>B\}$, $\ACT(B)=\{B>A_0\}$, and $s_Q=\deg Q$ if $X$ attains $D$ at
$q_Q=0$ (at a tie, the perturbed side attains it for $q_Q>0$) and $s_Q=0$ otherwise.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
\emph{The inner derivative is regime-independent.} The proof of
Theorem~\ref{thm:firstvar} uses only the inner $\max(A,B)$: the $1$-Lipschitz
dominator, the a.e.\ limit, and the finiteness of $K=\{A_0=B\}$. It never references
$\dA,\dB$ or which attains $D$. Hence
$\Phi^{+\prime}(0)=\int_{\ACT(X)}\log|Q(\chi)|\,ds$ in every regime (with $\ACT(B)$
for a $B$-side block, the inner branches swapped).

\emph{Quotient rule.} $D(0)\ge56>0$, and both $\Phi,D$ are right-differentiable at
$0$. For $f,g$ right-differentiable with $g(0)\ne0$,
$(f/g)^{+\prime}(0)=\bigl(f^{+\prime}(0)g(0)-f(0)g^{+\prime}(0)\bigr)/g(0)^2$, needing
no two-sided differentiability. With $f=\Phi$, $g=D$, $\Phi(0)/D=\log h$ this is
\eqref{eq:unified} once $s_Q=\dplus D/\partial q_Q|_0$ is identified.

\emph{The denominator's one-sided derivative.} For a max of affine functions the
right derivative is the largest slope over the active index set
\citep[Thm.~23.4]{Roc70}. Only the side $Q$ joined has nonzero slope $\deg Q$; so in
regimes I, II it equals $\deg Q$ if that side attains $D$ and $0$ otherwise. In
regime III both sides attain $D(0)$, and the right derivative is $\max(\deg Q,0)=\deg
Q$: the perturbed side becomes the unique attainer for $q_Q>0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The tie is a genuine kink]\label{rem:kink}
In regime III $\log h$ is not two-sided differentiable. For $q_Q\to0^-$ the
unperturbed side attains $D$, so $D$ has left derivative $0$ and $\log h$ has left
derivative $\Phi^{+\prime}(0)/D$ (no cross-term), while the right derivative carries
$-\log h\cdot\deg Q/D$. The two differ by exactly $\log h\cdot\deg Q/D$. Since
admissible perturbations have $q_Q\ge0$, the right derivative is the operative one and
the firing test is its sign. A naive envelope computation that ignores this kink
mispredicts at a tie; \eqref{eq:unified} does not.
\end{remark}

The two cases of \eqref{eq:unified} recover the marginals used in practice: a base
block ($s_Q=0$) gives $\frac1D\int_{\{A_0>B\}}\log|Q|$, the cross-term-free form of
Theorem~\ref{thm:firstvar}; a perturbing block ($s_Q=\deg Q$) gives
$\frac1D\bigl[\int_{\{B>A_0\}}\log|Q|-\log h\cdot\deg Q\bigr]$.

%==============================================================================
\section{The selection criterion}\label{sec:criterion}
%==============================================================================

Throughout this section the perturbing branch attains $D$ (the regime of every family
we construct). The two firing tests of Corollary~\ref{cor:criterion} are the two
cases of \eqref{eq:unified} read off by the side $Q$ joins.

\begin{definition}\label{def:rq}
Let an admissible family have base active set $\{A_0>B\}$ and perturbing active set
$\Omega=\{B>A_0\}$. For a nonzero $Q\in\Z[X]$:
\[
  \widetilde r_Q:=\int_{\{A_0>B\}}\log|Q(\chi(s))|\,ds
  \qquad(\text{base marginal}),\qquad
  \rQ:=\frac1{\deg Q}\int_\Omega\log|Q(\chi(s))|\,ds
  \qquad(\text{normalized active potential}).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:criterion}]
\emph{Base block} ($X=A$): since the perturbing branch attains $D$, a base block
enters the losing argument, so $s_Q=0$ in \eqref{eq:unified} and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:base-marg}
  \dplus\!\log h/\partial q_Q\big|_0=\frac1D\int_{\{A_0>B\}}\log|Q|
  =\frac{\widetilde r_Q}{D},
\end{equation}
with no $\log h$ term; firing is $\widetilde r_Q<0$.

\emph{Perturbing block} ($X=B$): here $Q$ enters the $D$-attaining argument, so
$s_Q=\deg Q$ and $\Phi^{+\prime}(0)=\int_\Omega\log|Q|=(\deg Q)\rQ$, giving
\begin{equation}\label{eq:marginal}
  \dplus\!\log h/\partial q_Q\big|_0=\frac{\deg Q}{D}\bigl(\rQ-\log h\bigr),
\end{equation}
firing iff $\rQ<\log h$. The optimality statements are the Karush--Kuhn--Tucker
conditions for the minimum over $q_Q\ge0$: an interior active perturbing block has
vanishing derivative, hence $\rQ=\log h$; one held at $q_Q=0$ has nonnegative
derivative, hence $\rQ\ge\log h$. In the dual of the \citet[\S6]{BMQS} measure program
restricted to Doche's family, $\rQ-\log h$ is the reduced cost of the block, so these
are its complementary-slackness conditions.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[The criterion is a first variation, so the anchor matters]\label{rem:anchor}
Both $\widetilde r_Q$ and $\rQ$ are evaluated at the family \emph{before} $Q$ is
adjoined; the firing test is for an admissible perturbation $q_Q\ge0$ from that anchor.
A family that already contains $Q$ has a different active set and reverses the reading,
and an unconstrained reoptimization driving $q_Q<0$ leaves the admissible cone and
certifies nothing. The sign test is the right derivative on the candidate-free anchor.
\end{remark}

\section{The improved bound}\label{sec:bound}
%==============================================================================

The base criterion selects two further admissible firing blocks from Flammang's
\citep{F18} table, the low-degree entries of degrees $3$ and $8$ (call them $j_3,j_9$),
each with negative base marginal $\widetilde r_Q<0$ at the held family
$Q_1Q_2\,Q_5^{q_E}Q_6^{q_F}$ (whose perturbing blocks $Q_5,Q_6$ in turn sit on
$\rQ=\log h$). The enriched family is
$h=Q_1Q_2\,Q_5^{q_E}Q_6^{q_F}\,j_3^{q_G}j_9^{q_H}$ with the nine exponents jointly
re-optimized to
\[
  q=(14.011500,13.443930,2.643590,2.299880,0.252420),\quad
  q_E=0.575080,\ q_F=0.568800,\ q_G=0.891590,\ q_H=0.066860,
\]
giving $D=\max(\dA,\dB)=\max(61.65784,72.00176)=72.00176$, the perturbing branch
attaining $D$. Admissibility holds by Lemma~\ref{lem:doche}: $j_9$ is irreducible
hence squarefree, distinct from $j_3$, coprime to $j_3$, and the active dictionary
$\{P_1,P_2,P_4,P_6,P_8,j_3,j_9\}\cup\{Q_1,Q_2,Q_5,Q_6\}$ is pairwise coprime; base
blocks need not satisfy $P(0)=P(1)=1$. Setting $q_H=0$ recovers the previous family
identically, confirming the new block extends a valid bound rather than altering the
integrand.

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:upper}]
By Lemma~\ref{lem:doche} it remains to enclose $\log h=\Phi/D$ from above. We bound
$\Phi=\int_0^1\max(A,B)\,ds$ by an adaptive quadrature with directed (outward)
rounding, without splitting the maximum: on each cell three guaranteed upper bounds on
$\int_{\text{cell}}\max(A,B)\,ds$ are taken in minimum---a \emph{flat} bound
$(b-a)\max(\sup A,\sup B)$ (never vacuous, since a near-zero of a block sends one
branch to $-\infty$ but the finite branch caps the maximum), an $O(h^2)$
\emph{straddle} bound from $\max(a+x,b+y)\le\max(a,b)+\max(x,y)$ applied to the
midpoint Taylor deviation, and an $O(h^3)$ \emph{midpoint} bound where one branch
dominates---and cells exceeding a deviation cap are bisected, only leaves contributing,
with exact tiling. Every cell bound and the running sum are rounded toward $+\infty$,
giving an upper bound $S\ge\int_0^{2\pi}\max(A,B)\,dt$. The normalizers are divisors of
this numerator, so to keep the quotient an over-estimate they are rounded toward
$-\infty$: a value $\underline{2\pi}\le2\pi$ and a value $\underline D\le D$ obtained by
rounding each nonnegative product $q_k\deg Q_k$ and partial sum of the affine
$D$-formula \eqref{eq:Dformula} downward (the exponents being nonnegative). Then
$\log h=\Phi/D\le S/(\underline{2\pi}\,\underline D)$, with the final division rounded up.
The frontier resolves fully ($742266$ leaves, none unresolved), $S/\underline{2\pi}\le
18.2804777610$ and $\underline D=72.00176$, whence
\[
  \essmin\;\le\;\log h\;\le\;\frac{18.2804777610}{72.00176}=0.2538893183\;<\;0.25443677. \qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

A soundness check compares the per-cell bound against a high-precision reference on
random cells with no violations on the safe side, and a deliberately understated target
is correctly rejected; the independent floating-point value $0.2538891201$ sits just
below the certified enclosure, the correct (outward) direction.

The improvement is modest and is not the record (Table~\ref{tab:gap}); its role is to
certify that the criterion selects blocks predictively, not to settle the constant. The
current record, $0.2536331090$ of \citet{Gri26}, is lower still---and, as the next
section shows, is itself selected block-for-block by the same criterion.

\section{The criterion accounts for the record}\label{sec:record}
%==============================================================================

The criterion is not only a design rule; it is a diagnostic, and we test it against a
construction it did not produce. The current record \citep{Gri26},
$\essmin\le0.2536331090$, is reached by a \emph{denominator-first} search: one fixes a
denominator $Q=Q_1Q_2R_0R_2P_7P_9$ of degree $140$ and optimizes the numerator
exponents $q_i$ over $P_1,\dots,P_6,P_8$ by linear programming, where
\begin{equation}\label{eq:R0R2}
  R_0=Q_1-P_1^5P_2^5P_4P_8,\qquad R_2=Q_2+P_1^5P_2^5P_4P_7
\end{equation}
are two \emph{near-cancellation} factors built to sit close to $Q_1,Q_2$. The blocks
$P_7,P_9$ are the degree-$12$ and degree-$16$ Flammang entries that appear as $Q_5,Q_6$
in this paper; $R_0,R_2$ are new and lie outside the dictionary of
Section~\ref{sec:bound}.

\subsection*{The same functional} The record bound is reported as
$\bigl(\log M(Q)+\int_0^1(A-B)^+\,ds\bigr)/\deg Q$ with $A=\sum_iq_i\log|P_i(\chi)|$
the numerator and $B=\log|Q(\chi)|$ the denominator. This is exactly Doche's
\eqref{eq:ratio} in complementary coordinates: by Jensen $\int_0^1 B\,ds=\log M(Q)$, so
\begin{equation}\label{eq:samefunctional}
  \frac{\log M(Q)+\int_0^1(A-B)^+\,ds}{\deg Q}
  =\frac{\int_0^1\bigl(B+(A-B)^+\bigr)ds}{\deg Q}
  =\frac{\int_0^1\max(A,B)\,ds}{\deg Q}=\frac{\Phi}{D}=\log h.
\end{equation}
The two constructions optimize one surface; the denominator-first search descends it
where the first-variation calculus differentiates it. Here the perturbing branch is
$B$, so $\Omega=\{B>A_0\}$ is the perturbing active set and
Corollary~\ref{cor:criterion} applies block by block.

\begin{theorem}[The record satisfies the firing criterion]\label{thm:record-fires}
At the candidate-free anchor---the record family with one block removed---each of the
six denominator blocks $Q_1,Q_2,R_0,R_2,P_7,P_9$ fires by the perturbing test
$\rQ<\log h$ of Corollary~\ref{cor:criterion}; for the two new factors
\[
  \rQ(R_0)=-0.0688,\qquad \rQ(R_2)=-0.0656,
\]
far below $\log h=0.25363$, and likewise $\rQ(P_7)=-0.0158$, $\rQ(P_9)=-0.0411$. The
test is discriminating: generic integer blocks of comparable degree do not fire---
$\rQ(P_3)=0.272$, $\rQ(P_5)=0.264$, $\rQ(X^2-X+1)=0.276$, all exceeding $\log h$.
Moreover at the record's own optimum the active blocks lie on the stationarity locus of
the criterion: $\rQ-\log h$ for all six clusters in $[+0.0069,+0.0084]$, spread
$0.0015$, the complementary-slackness signature of an interior optimum.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The identity \eqref{eq:samefunctional} is exact. The marginals are
$\rQ=\frac1{\deg Q}\int_\Omega\log|Q(\chi)|\,ds$ of Definition~\ref{def:rq}, evaluated
on a fine midpoint grid; a computation (Section~\ref{sec:verify}) returns the stated
values, reproduces the record value $\log h=0.2536269$ from \eqref{eq:samefunctional}
to $10^{-9}$, and confirms the firing signs and the stationarity cluster. The
candidate-free reading is the correct one by Remark~\ref{rem:anchor}: each block's
marginal is the right derivative of $\log h$ as that block enters at $q_Q=0^+$.
\end{proof}

The reading is \emph{explanatory}, not generative: the criterion does not produce
$R_0,R_2$---those are the near-cancellation construction \eqref{eq:R0R2} of
\citet{Gri26}---but it certifies after the fact that they fire, that generic blocks do
not, and that the record sits at the criterion's first-order optimum. The small uniform
offset $\rQ-\log h\approx+0.007$ rather than $0$ is consistent with the denominator
exponents being held at multiplicity one rather than driven to interior stationarity;
the entry test $\rQ<\log h$, which is what selects the blocks, holds with wide margin.
A search that knows the criterion would have proposed exactly these blocks; the record
is the calculus carried out numerically.

\section{Where the reachable gains run out}\label{sec:saturation}
%==============================================================================

With the bound in hand we read the base criterion of
Corollary~\ref{cor:criterion} the other way---as a design objective---and ask which
integer blocks improve it. The blocks of Section~\ref{sec:bound} enter the base branch,
so the relevant quantity is the base marginal
$\widetilde r_Q=\int_{\{A_0>B\}}\log|Q(\chi)|\,ds$, with $Q$ firing iff
$\widetilde r_Q<0$. The results of this section are \emph{computational evidence} over
an explicit finite search, not a non-existence theorem; we are explicit about the
search's boundaries.

The one structural fact is additivity. Since
$\widetilde r_Q=\int_{\{A_0>B\}}\log|Q|\,ds$ is an integral of $\log|Q|=\sum\log|Q_i|$
over a fixed arc,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:additive}
  \widetilde r_{Q_1Q_2}=\widetilde r_{Q_1}+\widetilde r_{Q_2}.
\end{equation}
A reducible block fires only if it has a firing factor.

\begin{remark}[The cheap firing blocks are already in the dictionary]\label{rem:evidence}
Evaluated at the certified family of Section~\ref{sec:bound} on the candidate-free
anchor, an enumeration of Flammang's \citep{F18} Table~1 finds the firing entries
($\widetilde r_Q<0$) and sorts them by whether they are coprime to the active
dictionary $W=\{P_1,P_2,P_4,P_6,P_8,j_3,j_9,Q_1,Q_2,Q_5,Q_6\}$. The low-degree firing
entries that are \emph{not} coprime---degrees $1,3,12,12,16$---each share a factor with
$W$ and so are already absorbed; the four cheapest firing factors are exactly the base
blocks $j_3,j_9$ and the perturbing blocks $Q_5,Q_6$ used in the bound. Three
higher-degree firing table entries (degrees $16,16,20$, base marginals $\approx-0.005$
to $-0.008$) \emph{are} coprime to $W$: these would fire, but their realized gain after
re-optimization falls below the certificate's resolution (Section~\ref{sec:bound}), so
adjoining them does not yield a further certified break. A direct closest-vector and
root-target search over original integer blocks (degree $\le10$, bounded coefficients;
roughly $7.4\times10^4$ candidates, extended to degree $16$ near the firing locus)
returns no original block whose base marginal is more negative than these, and by
\eqref{eq:additive} a reducible block built from a coprime factor of cost
$\widetilde r\ge+0.013$ (the cheapest such factor found, $X^2-X+1$) cannot beat the
best firing margin $\approx-0.005$. The evidence is therefore that the cheap,
\emph{certifiably} improving blocks of this construction have been absorbed---not that
no admissible block of any degree fires.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Why the cheap firing set is so thin]\label{rem:wells}
The firing integer blocks are isolated in the coefficient lattice: a single $\pm1$
change to a firing table entry swings its base marginal from $\approx-0.005$ to above
$+0.06$. Geometrically, $\widetilde r_Q$ is negative only when a root of $Q$ sits in a
deep well of the base active-arc potential, and those wells lie strictly inside the
lemniscate $\chi(\{|z|<1\})$, where an admissible integer block cannot place a root
without a conjugate elsewhere that pays for it. This is the mechanism behind the
thinness; the statements above rest on the additivity \eqref{eq:additive} and the
reproduced marginals, not on the geometry.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Not a transfinite-diameter problem]\label{rem:noteq}
Factoring $\log|Q|$ over the roots writes $\rQ$ as an averaged logarithmic potential
of $\Omega$, which invites reading block selection as an equilibrium
(transfinite-diameter) problem. It is not: the equilibrium measure of $\Omega$ has
constant potential $\approx0.524$ on its support, well above $\log h=0.254$, so
$\Omega$'s measure is not the equilibrium measure and $\log h$ is not its Robin
constant. The integer constraint, not capacity, controls the value, and the correct
frame is the first-variation calculus above.
\end{remark}
