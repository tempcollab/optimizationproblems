A 63-dimensional counterexample to Borsuk’s conjecture

Max Grinsztajn
grinsztajnmax@gmail.com

May 2026

Abstract

We give a ﬁnite construction showing that Borsuk’s conjecture fails in dimension 63.

Equivalently, if dB denotes the least dimension in which Borsuk’s conjecture fails, then

dB ≤ 63.

The construction is a 321-point set X ⊂ R63 such that every subset of strictly smaller
diameter has at most 5 points. Hence any partition of X into smaller-diameter subsets
requires at least ⌈321/5⌉ = 65 > 64 parts. The construction starts from the standard
G2(4) representation used by Bondarenko and Jenrich–Brouwer; the new step is to take a
320-point subconﬁguration in a codimension-two subspace and adjoin one projected point.

1 Introduction

Borsuk’s conjecture asks whether every bounded set in Rd can be partitioned into d + 1 subsets
of strictly smaller diameter. Let dB denote the least dimension in which the conjecture fails.
Bondarenko’s construction from the G2(4) graph gave dB ≤ 65 [1], and the Jenrich–Brouwer
reﬁnement gave dB ≤ 64 [2].

We prove the following improvement.

Theorem 1. Borsuk’s conjecture fails in dimension 63. In particular,

dB ≤ 63.

The only external input is the standard G2(4) graph construction and its usual Euclidean
representation. The additional ﬁnite facts about the partition used here are checked by the
veriﬁcation script in the accompanying repository [4]. From these facts we prove two new steps:
a 320-point subconﬁguration lies in a codimension-two subspace of the usual 65-dimensional
representation, and one can adjoin a projected point in this subspace without allowing any
smaller-diameter subset to have more than 5 points.

7 Computational veriﬁcation

The ﬁnite checks in Lemma 1 are reproduced by the veriﬁcation script in the accompanying
repository [4]. The script reconstructs the graph from PG(2, 16), veriﬁes the strongly regular
parameters (416, 100, 36, 20), builds the partition B1, B2, B3, C, checks the degree data used in
Lemma 3, and checks the clique obstruction used in Lemma 6. It also checks that the selected
vertex b has no 5-clique inside N (b) ∩ C; this last check is a consistency check and is not needed
for the proof above.

The script is deterministic and uses exact ﬁnite-ﬁeld arithmetic and integer bitsets.
